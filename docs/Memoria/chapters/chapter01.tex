\chapter{Introduction}\label{chapter:Intro} % la etiqueta para referencias
For decades, traditional financial networks have been the backbone of the global financial system. These networks, organized around intermediaries such as banks or firms, enable individuals and businesses to conduct transactions and manage resources. However, they exhibit significant limitations. The reliance on intermediaries increases transaction costs and often reduces transparency, as transaction details are not easily accessible to all participants. Furthermore, trust in these networks depends heavily on the reputation and practices of intermediaries, which may not always guarantee security and efficiency. For example, the \citeA{world_bank_remittance_2024} estimates that global remittance fees averaged 6.35\% in 2024, still far from the G20 commitment to reduce the global average to 3 percent. traditional banks remain the most expensive type of service provider, with an average cost of 12.66\%, underscoring the inefficiencies in traditional financial systems.

Blockchain technology has emerged as a promising solution to these challenges. In a blockchain network, transactions are recorded in a decentralized manner, eliminating the need for intermediaries and potentially reducing costs while improving efficiency. Moreover, each transaction is logged on a “ledger” accessible to all network participants, enabling full traceability and simplifying information verification. This transparency and traceability introduce a new paradigm of trust, where individuals and businesses can rely on the system itself rather than on centralized intermediaries.

Despite its potential, blockchain adoption in financial networks is not without challenges. Maintaining a decentralized network incurs infrastructure costs, and the technology introduces unique security risks. Also, blockchain may have positive and negative network effects. These challenges raise critical questions: Can blockchain completely replace traditional networks? Will traditional systems retain their role in the financial system? Alternatively, is it possible for both systems to coexist, leveraging their respective strengths to reach a financial equilibrium?


\section{Hypothesis}
Financial agents optimize their transaction allocation by choosing between traditional financial systems and blockchain-based systems, based on factors such as transaction costs, transparency, and security. Under certain conditions, the benefits of blockchain will allow it to co-exist with traditional systems. However, in contexts of high intermediation and lack of transparency in traditional systems, blockchain may become the preferred choice.


\section{Goals}
\subsection{Main goal}
%nuevo objetivo
The primary objective of this thesis is to develop and analyze a theoretical optimization model that explains how financial agents allocate their transactions between traditional financial systems and blockchain-based systems. By integrating key factors such as transaction costs, security risks, institutional trust, and network effects, this research aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of the decision-making processes that guide these allocations.

The model will explore the interactions between both systems, capturing the interdependent risks and the influence of network growth within blockchain. This study will seek to identify the conditions under which financial agents reach an optimal equilibrium, balancing their use of blockchain and traditional systems, and will investigate whether both systems can sustainably coexist or if one will ultimately prevail.

Ultimately, this research aims to offer insight into the future landscape of financial systems, shed light on how blockchain technologies may complement or challenge traditional structures, and provide a robust framework for understanding the economic and strategic implications for financial agents and regulators.

\subsection{Specific goals}

The specific goals of this thesis are to:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Develop a utility-based model that represents how financial agents allocate transactions between traditional financial systems and blockchain systems. This model will incorporate transaction costs, security risks, trust in institutions, and network effects to capture the agent decision-making process.

\item Analyze the role of transaction costs in both systems, determining how the costs associated with traditional intermediaries and blockchain networks affect the optimal allocation of financial resources by agents.

\item Investigate the influence of security and regulatory risks in both financial environments. The model will include risk factors such as the likelihood of hacking in blockchain systems and institutional risks in traditional systems, aiming to understand how agents manage these risks in their transaction decisions.

\item Evaluate the impact of network effects on blockchain systems, especially how the growth of the blockchain network can enhance security, reduce costs, and increase liquidity, but potentially slow it down.

\item Explore the interdependence of risks between blockchain and traditional financial systems, focusing on how increased risks in one system may affect the stability, costs, or adoption of the other system.

\item Derive conditions for equilibrium in which financial agents reach a stable allocation of transactions between the two systems, or alternatively, identify the circumstances in which one system may dominate over the other.

\item Provide a comprehensive framework that explains the economic and strategic implications of blockchain adoption, offering insights into whether blockchain will complement or disrupt traditional financial systems, and under what conditions each system can thrive.

\item Understand how information spreads and social learning occurs with respect to blockchain
technologies.

\item Assess how cryptocurrency price uncertainty affects the provision of blockchain net-
works as public goods.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Document structure}
This thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 2 provides a comprehensive bibliographic review, covering traditional financial networks, blockchain, and general equilibrium models. Chapter 3 presents the theoretical framework and outlines the proposed utility function that captures agents' decision-making processes. Chapter 4 discusses the conditions for equilibrium and analyzes the stability of the proposed model. Finally, Chapter 5 summarizes the findings, discusses their implications, and suggests  future research.
